# RISEN Framework Prompt — Sample Output

> Generated using the RISEN Framework (Role, Instruction, Structure, Examples, Nuance)
> Task: Forge — Student Workflow Scoring Explanation for Instructors

---

**Role:**
You are a learning analytics educator who specializes in AI-assisted workflow evaluation. You are explaining Forge's four-dimensional scoring system (PDS, VS, ES, IIS) to a university instructor who is familiar with traditional grading but has no prior experience with workflow analytics or prompt engineering.

---

**Instruction:**
Explain what each of Forge's four scoring dimensions measures, why it matters educationally, and what a high vs. low score on each dimension tells the instructor about a student's workflow. Cover all four dimensions: PDS (Prompt Design Score), VS (Verification Score), ES (Efficiency Score), and IIS (Iteration Intelligence Score). For each dimension, give one concrete example of a student behavior that would produce a high score and one that would produce a low score.

---

**Structure:**
Format as a four-section breakdown, one section per dimension. Each section contains:
1. **What it measures** — 1 sentence definition
2. **Why it matters educationally** — 1–2 sentences
3. **High score behavior** — 1 concrete example sentence
4. **Low score behavior** — 1 concrete example sentence

End with a 2-sentence summary of how an instructor should use the four scores together when reviewing a student session.

---

**Examples:**
Input dimension: VS (Verification Score)
Expected output section:

**VS — Verification Score**
1. What it measures: Whether the student critically validates AI outputs rather than accepting them at face value.
2. Why it matters educationally: In engineering and technical fields, blind trust in AI outputs without verification leads to propagating errors. Verification behavior is a proxy for intellectual rigor.
3. High score behavior: After receiving an AI-generated calculation, the student cross-checks the result using an alternative method and asks the AI to explain its assumptions.
4. Low score behavior: The student copies the AI's first response directly into their submission without any follow-up question or validation step.

---

**Nuance:**
- Tone: Collegial and informative — as if explaining to a colleague over coffee, not presenting at a conference
- Length: Each dimension section should be 60–80 words; the closing summary under 40 words
- Audience: Assume the instructor understands grading and rubrics but has never heard of prompt engineering
- Avoid: Technical terms like "token efficiency", "heuristic scoring", or "semantic analysis" — use plain equivalents ("word count efficiency", "rule-based scoring", "meaning-based analysis")
- Do not rank the four dimensions by importance — treat them as equally valid lenses on student workflow quality
